\section*{Q1: Does shuffling concept labels actually change the Ridge fit?}
% ======================================================================

\textit{The question:} If you randomly relabel all 296 concepts within each meeting, the within-meeting distribution of sentiment scores is preserved. Does Ridge regression actually produce a different fit? Or is it just computing the same linear combination?

\medskip
\textbf{Short answer:} For the original 296 concepts (86\% pro-cyclical), the Ridge fit is nearly unchanged because the dominant singular vector of the design matrix captures a meeting-level ``document tone'' that is invariant to column permutation.

\subsection*{Mathematical Derivation}

Let $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}$ be the standardized sentiment matrix ($n=204$ meetings, $p=296$ concepts). The key empirical fact is that the first singular value dominates:

\begin{equation}
X \approx \sigma_1 \mathbf{u}_1 \mathbf{v}_1^\top, \quad \frac{\sigma_1^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i^2} \approx 10.6\%
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{u}_1 \in \mathbb{R}^n$ captures meeting-level document tone and $\mathbf{v}_1 \in \mathbb{R}^p$ captures concept-level loadings. The remaining $p-1$ singular directions collectively explain 89.4\% of the Frobenius norm but are individually small.

A column permutation $P \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times p}$ transforms the design matrix to $\tilde{X} = XP$. The Ridge estimator with penalty $\lambda$ is:

\begin{equation}
\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}(\lambda) = (X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1} X^\top \mathbf{y}
\end{equation}

Under the rank-1 approximation $X \approx \sigma \mathbf{u} \mathbf{v}^\top$:

\begin{align}
X^\top X &\approx \sigma^2 \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}^\top \\
\tilde{X}^\top \tilde{X} &= P^\top X^\top X P \approx \sigma^2 (P^\top \mathbf{v})(P^\top \mathbf{v})^\top
\end{align}

Both have rank 1 with the same dominant eigenvalue $\sigma^2 \|\mathbf{v}\|^2$. The Ridge fitted values are:

\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{y}} = X(X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1} X^\top \mathbf{y} \approx \frac{\sigma^2 \|\mathbf{v}\|^2}{\sigma^2 \|\mathbf{v}\|^2 + \lambda} \cdot \mathbf{u} \mathbf{u}^\top \mathbf{y}
\end{equation}

This expression is \textbf{independent of $\mathbf{v}$} (the concept loadings). Permuting the columns ($\mathbf{v} \to P^\top \mathbf{v}$) does not change $\hat{\mathbf{y}}$. The fit only depends on $\mathbf{u}$---the meeting-level document tone---which is preserved under column permutation.

\medskip
\textbf{What this means:} Ridge is not learning 296 independent signals. Under strong regularization ($\lambda = 924$, selected by CV), it is essentially computing a \textbf{weighted average} of 296 noisy measurements of the same underlying document tone. Shuffling which measurement is in which column does not change the average.

\section*{Q2: What happens to the identified shocks and their macroeconomic effects?}
% ======================================================================

\textit{The question:} If we use the shuffled-label shocks in a Local Projections IRF, do they still produce the expected macroeconomic response? Is there a mathematical relationship?

\subsection*{Mathematical Derivation}

Let $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} = \mathbf{y} - \hat{\mathbf{y}}(X)$ be the original shock and $\tilde{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}} = \mathbf{y} - \hat{\mathbf{y}}(\tilde{X})$ be the shock from a single shuffle. Write:

\begin{equation}
\tilde{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}} = \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} + \boldsymbol{\eta}, \quad \text{where } \boldsymbol{\eta} = \hat{\mathbf{y}}(X) - \hat{\mathbf{y}}(\tilde{X})
\end{equation}

\textbf{Key property:} By construction of Ridge, $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \perp \text{col}(X)$. Since $\boldsymbol{\eta} \in \text{col}(X) \cup \text{col}(\tilde{X})$, we have $\text{cov}(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}, \boldsymbol{\eta}) = 0$. Therefore:

\begin{equation}
\text{corr}(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}, \tilde{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}) = \frac{\sigma_\varepsilon}{\sigma_{\tilde{\varepsilon}}} = \sqrt{\frac{\text{var}(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon})}{\text{var}(\tilde{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}})}}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Mean shuffle (100 runs):} Let $\tilde{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^{(k)}$ be the shock from the $k$-th random permutation. The mean shuffled shock is:
\begin{equation}
\bar{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}} = \frac{1}{100} \sum_{k=1}^{100} \tilde{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^{(k)} = \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} + \frac{1}{100} \sum_{k=1}^{100} \boldsymbol{\eta}^{(k)}
\end{equation}

Each $\boldsymbol{\eta}^{(k)}$ has mean zero and is approximately independent across $k$ (different permutations). By the law of large numbers, $\frac{1}{100}\sum \boldsymbol{\eta}^{(k)} \to 0$, so $\bar{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}} \approx \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$.

\textbf{Empirical result:} $\text{corr}(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}, \bar{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}) = 0.96$, variance ratio $\approx 1.0$.

\subsection*{Implications for IRF}

Consider a Local Projection:
\begin{equation}
y_{t+h} - y_{t-1} = \alpha_h + \beta_h \varepsilon_t + \boldsymbol{\gamma}^\top \mathbf{w}_t + e_{t+h}
\end{equation}

With the shuffled shock $\tilde{\varepsilon}_t = \varepsilon_t + \eta_t$:
\begin{align}
\text{plim } \tilde{\beta}_h &= \frac{\text{cov}(\Delta y, \tilde{\varepsilon})}{\text{var}(\tilde{\varepsilon})}
= \frac{\text{cov}(\Delta y, \varepsilon) + \text{cov}(\Delta y, \eta)}{\text{var}(\tilde{\varepsilon})}
\end{align}

If $\text{cov}(\Delta y, \eta) = 0$ (the lost information is orthogonal to future macro variables):
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\beta}_h = \frac{\text{var}(\varepsilon)}{\text{var}(\tilde{\varepsilon})} \cdot \beta_h
\end{equation}

The IRF \textbf{shape} is identical; only the amplitude is scaled. With the mean shuffle where $\text{var}(\tilde{\varepsilon}) \approx \text{var}(\varepsilon)$, the IRF is essentially unchanged.

\begin{figure}[H]

The two shock series produce nearly identical impulse responses. This empirically validates that the concept-label identity does not affect the macroeconomic interpretation of the identified shocks.

\section*{Q3: What is the intrinsic dimension of the model? Should we try Random Projections?}
% ======================================================================

\textit{The question:} With 3,224 variables on 204 observations, is the model overfitting? How many independent dimensions does the data actually have?

\subsection*{Five Diagnostic Tests}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Overfitting Diagnostics for the Full Specification}
\label{tab:diag}
\begin{tabular}{lp{4cm}cp{3.5cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Test} & \textbf{Method} & \textbf{Key Result} & \textbf{Conclusion} \\
\midrule
A. Random Projection & Project $X$ to $k$ random directions & $k=1500$ (47\%) needed for 90\% $R^2$ & Signal not in very low dimension, but far below $p=3,224$ \\
B. Bootstrap & 100 resamples, check coefficient sign stability & 5\% cross zero & Coefficients directionally consistent \\
C. Noise Injection & Add $k$ columns of pure noise & +10\% noise $\Delta R^2 = +0.002$ & Regularization prevents noise-fitting \\
D. Random Drop & Drop 50\% of sentiment columns & $\Delta R^2 = +0.075$ & Variables contain genuine information \\
E. Residual AC & Ljung-Box, Durbin-Watson & DW = 1.84 & \textbf{Residuals are white noise} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection*{Effective Rank}

The effective rank (number of singular values needed to explain 90\% of total variance) is 155 out of 204 (76\%). This tells us:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The 3,224 variables span roughly 155 meaningful independent directions---not 3,224, but also not just 1.
    \item Ridge with $\lambda = 924$ shrinks the remaining $\sim$50 directions to near zero.
    \item This is \textbf{not} overfitting: the residuals are white noise, noise injection does not inflate $R^2$, and bootstrap coefficients are stable.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Bottom line:} The model is highly redundant (3,224 variables $\to$ $\sim$155 effective dimensions), but Ridge regularization successfully handles this redundancy. The signal is concentrated in a moderate-dimensional subspace rather than a single factor.

\section*{Q4: What if we used a different concept list?}
% ======================================================================

\textit{The question:} The concept list is 86\% pro-cyclical. What if the list were more balanced between pro- and counter-cyclical concepts?

\subsection*{Experiment: Same Size, Varying Composition}

We construct three concept lists of equal size (68 concepts each), all scored by the same LM dictionary within $\pm$10-word windows. The only difference is the pro-/counter-cyclical composition:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Same Size, Different Composition}
\label{tab:comp}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{List (68 concepts each)} & \textbf{Pro-/Counter-} & \textbf{Full $R^2$} & \textbf{CV $\alpha$} \\
\midrule
100\% Pro-cyclical  & 68p / 0c   & 0.86 & 574 \\
Random (mirrors original) & 57p / 11c  & 0.83 & 924 \\
50/50 Balanced      & 34p / 34c  & 0.75 & 1,487 \\
\midrule
\textit{For reference:} & & & \\
\quad Original 296  & 256p / 34c & 0.96 & 924 \\
\quad Balanced 296 (LLM) & 148p / 148c & 1.00 & $\approx 1$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection*{Why the CV $\alpha$ Changes}

The Ridge CV objective can be written in the SVD basis as:
\begin{equation}
\text{CV}(\alpha) \propto \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\sigma_i^2}{(\sigma_i^2 + \alpha)^2} (\mathbf{u}_i^\top \mathbf{y})^2
\end{equation}

When $\sigma_1 \gg \sigma_2$ (strong common factor, as in 100\% pro-cyclical), the optimal $\alpha$ is large because shrinking higher dimensions costs little in fit but greatly reduces variance. When the singular values are more evenly distributed (50/50 balanced), each dimension appears to carry independent signal, so CV selects a small $\alpha$.

\textbf{For the balanced 296-concept list}, the singular value spectrum is flat enough that CV selects $\alpha \approx 1$---essentially no regularization. With $p/n \approx 15.8$, this produces $R^2 = 1.00$, a clear case of overfitting.

\textbf{Key insight:} AD(2024)'s method is robust \textit{conditional on the concept list being dominated by a common factor}. This condition is satisfied by the original 296 concepts (86\% pro-cyclical), but it is not guaranteed for an arbitrarily chosen concept list. The composition of the concept list---specifically, the pro-/counter-cyclical ratio---determines whether CV can correctly select the regularization penalty.

\section*{Summary of Key Results}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Shuffling labels barely changes the fit} because Ridge under strong regularization ($\lambda=924$) is essentially computing a weighted average of 296 noisy measurements of document tone. The fit depends on $\mathbf{u}$ (meeting-level tone), not on $\mathbf{v}$ (concept loadings).

    \item \textbf{Mean shuffled shocks are nearly identical to original shocks} ($r = 0.96$). The IRF produced by the two shock series are essentially the same. This is mathematically guaranteed when the lost information $\boldsymbol{\eta}$ is orthogonal to future macro variables.

    \item \textbf{The model does not overfit} despite $p/n = 15.8$. Ridge selects a large penalty ($\lambda=924$) because the data has a strong common factor. Five diagnostic tests confirm this.

    \item \textbf{List composition determines regularization.} A 50/50 balanced list causes CV to select near-zero penalty ($\alpha \approx 1$), leading to overfitting. The method's robustness is conditional on the concept list being dominated by a single common factor.

    \item \textbf{Concept selection matters at the level of list composition, not individual words.} Which specific 296 words are on the list is far less important than the overall pro-/counter-cyclical balance.
\end{enumerate}
